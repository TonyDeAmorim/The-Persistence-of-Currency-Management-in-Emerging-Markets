# Learning to Float ? The Persistence of Currency Management in Emerging Markets : A Modern Perspective on Calvo and Reinhart (2002)
This project replicates and extends the foundational framework of Calvo and Reinhart (2002) to investigate the contemporary persistence of "Fear of Floating" in Emerging Market Economies (EMEs). By analyzing data from the 2010-2018 period, the study examines whether nations that officially adopt flexible exchange rate regimes continue to manage their currencies due to vulnerabilities like dollar-denominated debt and inflationary pressures. The essay introduces an endogenous variability threshold of 2.13%, derived from the 60% probability of monthly fluctuations in modern benchmark currencies (EUR, JPY, GBP, AUD), to replace the static 2.5% band used in earlier literature. The empirical analysis reveals a striking heterogeneity among EMEs, distinguishing between "Rigid Anchors" like Kenya and the Philippines, which maintain extreme stability, and "Progressive Flexibles" like Mexico and Chile, which allow for greater market-driven variance. Ultimately, the findings suggest that the Global Financial Cycle and US dollar dominance continue to constrain monetary autonomy, forcing many EMEs to manage fluctuations despite their official status as floaters.
